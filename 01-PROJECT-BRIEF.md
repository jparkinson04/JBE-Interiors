# JBE Interiors — Project Brief

> The north star document. Read this first. Every design and build decision should ladder back to what's in here.

---

## The client

**JBE Interiors Ltd** is a UK-based interior fit-out contractor specialising in restaurant and retail sectors. Operating nationwide with completed projects across the UK, Ireland, and Jersey. Notable clients include Wagamama's, Nando's, and independent hospitality brands like Parsons and Zelmans.

Run by **Jason Beedle, Managing Director**. The wider business is the JBE Group, which also includes JBE Developments — but this site is for **JBE Interiors specifically**.

Based in Doncaster, DN12TB. Phone 01302 322202. Email jason@jbeinteriors.com.

---

## The problem

The current site (jbeinteriors.com) is roughly seven years old. It's a single-page layout with a logo, contact details, and a small project carousel. The work behind it has grown well past what the site reflects.

Specific issues with the current site:

- **One page, no depth.** No project detail, no story behind each build, no sense of scale.
- **Project images are small and undifferentiated.** Each project is reduced to a single thumbnail and a name. Wagamama's gets the same treatment as a regional independent.
- **Large blank spaces** next to photos that could carry project context, scope, client name, location.
- **Instagram is mentioned but tucked away** at the bottom. The most recent and active marketing channel is treated as an afterthought.
- **The aesthetic is dated.** The dark background photo, mixed typography, and crowded layout don't match the standard of the interiors JBE actually builds.

New clients are now visiting the site as part of due diligence. The site is starting to do damage rather than help.

---

## What the new site needs to do

In order of priority:

1. **Showcase the work properly.** Each featured project deserves its own profile with imagery, written context, and visible client/location/scope.
2. **Signal scale and credibility.** Someone landing on this site for the first time should immediately understand this is a serious nationwide operator working with major hospitality brands.
3. **Pull Instagram to the front.** A live feed showing the four most recent posts, integrated on the landing page.
4. **Make contact obvious.** Phone, email, location, and social — clearly presented, easy to act on.
5. **Be straightforward to update.** Adding new projects shouldn't require Jessie every time (though there's a maintenance retainer for those who'd rather not).

---

## What it explicitly doesn't need

- Online booking. JBE works B2B; enquiries come through phone, email, or referral.
- E-commerce.
- Blog or news section.
- Multi-language support.
- Login / client portal.

If any of these creep into scope, push back.

---

## Audience

**Primary audience:** Senior decision-makers at hospitality/retail brands considering JBE for a fit-out contract. Operations directors, property leads, brand managers at restaurant chains, independents looking to expand. They're checking JBE out before a first conversation or before signing.

**What they want to know in ten seconds:**
- Is this a real, established operation?
- Have they done work at our scale before?
- What does their finished work actually look like?
- Who do I contact?

**What they don't want:**
- Sales copy.
- Promises about "transforming your vision."
- Buzzwords.

The site should answer their questions through evidence — finished projects, named clients, considered presentation — not through claims.

---

## Voice and tone

JBE is craft-led. The work is the point. The site should:

- **Let the imagery do the heavy lifting.** Restaurant interiors are visual by nature.
- **Use plain, confident language.** Short sentences. No agency-speak. No "we partner with you to bring your vision to life" energy.
- **Acknowledge scale without bragging.** "Projects across the UK, Ireland, and Jersey" is more powerful than "nationwide leaders in commercial fit-out solutions."
- **Trust the reader.** They know what a restaurant looks like; they don't need it explained.

A useful filter: would Jason actually say this out loud to a client over coffee? If no, rewrite it.

---

## Aesthetic direction

**Editorial. Confident. Photography-led.**

Reference points to think about (not to copy):
- The way **Soho House** presents its venues — full-bleed photography, generous whitespace, restrained typography.
- The way **Aēsop** treats product pages — single objects, considered framing, minimal language.
- The way **architecture studio websites** (Studio Reed, Tom Dixon, David Thulstrup) present completed projects.

What this is **not**:
- Not corporate-construction (no hard hats, no scaffolding hero shots).
- Not generic-portfolio (no grid of equal-sized tiles).
- Not over-designed (no parallax, no scroll-jacking, no animated reveals on every element).

The site should feel like the kind of brochure JBE would hand to a client at a meeting — quality stock, considered layout, photography that earns its space.

---

## Constraints

- **Three pages.** Landing, Projects, Contact. Don't add more.
- **4-week build.** Tight but achievable for this scope.
- **Mobile, tablet, desktop responsive.**
- **Built to update.** Project pages should follow a consistent template so new ones can be added without bespoke design work.
- **Live Instagram feed required** on the landing page (four most recent posts, auto-updating).

---

## Success looks like

- Jason can send the link to a prospective client and feel proud of it.
- A first-time visitor immediately understands the scale and quality of the work.
- Adding a new project takes 15 minutes, not 3 hours.
- The site doesn't need rebuilding again in 18 months.
